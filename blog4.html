How Spanish Conquered a Continent — And the Interpreters Who Made It Possible

By Jimmy Cabrera  ·  Puente Translations
Bridging Languages. Connecting Lives.

In 1492, Christopher Columbus sailed west looking for Asia and stumbled onto a world Europe didn't know existed. He brought three ships, a royal commission, and an Arabic-speaking interpreter.

That last detail tells you everything.

Because the moment a Spanish sailor set foot on Caribbean soil and a Taíno villager walked toward him across the beach — two worlds that had never spoken to each other, never imagined each other, never shared a single word — someone had to bridge that silence.

And for the next century, the history of an entire hemisphere turned, again and again, on the question of language.

✦ ✦ ✦

Columbus and the Interpreter Problem

Columbus's Arabic-speaking interpreter, a man named Luis de Torres, was famously useless on that first voyage. Arabic bore no relationship whatsoever to the Taíno languages of the Caribbean. The encounter between Europe and the Americas began, linguistically speaking, in total silence — two peoples staring at each other across an unbridgeable gap.

Columbus resorted to what explorers have always done in that situation: he took people. Several Taíno men and women were brought back to Spain on his return voyage, not as slaves initially, but as living language projects — to be taught Spanish so they could serve as interpreters on future voyages.

It was a brutal, pragmatic solution. And it set the pattern for everything that followed.

✦ ✦ ✦

The Interpreter as Weapon

By the time Hernán Cortés arrived on the Mexican coast in 1519, the Spanish had learned their lesson. Language was not an afterthought — it was a strategic asset, as important as cannons or cavalry.

Cortés acquired two interpreters who between them gave him something no European had ever possessed: a direct line into the heart of the Aztec empire.

The first was Gerónimo de Aguilar, a Spanish priest who had been shipwrecked on the Yucatán coast eight years earlier and had learned to speak Mayan fluently. The second was La Malinche — born into a Nahua-speaking family, sold into slavery, gifted to Cortés along with nineteen other women on the coast of Tabasco.

Together they formed a remarkable chain. When Cortés needed to speak to Aztec emissaries, he spoke Spanish to Aguilar, who translated into Mayan for La Malinche, who translated into Nahuatl for the Aztecs. Three languages, two intermediaries, one conversation — and through that conversation, an empire began to fall.

La Malinche eventually learned Spanish directly. The Spanish called her Doña Marina. The indigenous peoples called her La Lengua — literally, the tongue. There is no more powerful title in the history of translation.

✦ ✦ ✦

The Word That Conquered More Than Swords

Here is something the history books rarely emphasize: the Spanish conquest of the Americas was not won by military force alone. The Spanish were vastly outnumbered in every territory they entered. What gave them their decisive advantage was not gunpowder — it was alliance building, and alliance building required language.

Cortés didn't conquer the Aztec empire. He convinced dozens of peoples who resented Aztec dominance — the Tlaxcalans, the Totonacs, and many others — to fight alongside him. That required negotiation. That required persuasion. That required translation.

La Malinche negotiated those alliances. She warned Cortés of ambushes. She identified loyalties and treacheries. She understood the political landscape of Mesoamerica in a way no European could, and she communicated it to the man who used that knowledge to reshape a continent.

The same pattern played out in Peru. Francisco Pizarro relied on an interpreter named Felipillo to communicate with the Inca emperor Atahualpa. Their famous meeting at Cajamarca in 1532 — which ended with Atahualpa's capture and the beginning of the end of the Inca empire — was conducted entirely through an interpreter. Some historians argue that Felipillo's translation contributed directly to the violence that followed. The words spoken in that plaza, filtered through one man's interpretation, may have cost millions of lives.

✦ ✦ ✦

The Missionaries and the Making of New Languages

Not all of the language work of the conquest was military. As the Spanish empire consolidated its hold on the Americas, a remarkable group of Franciscan, Dominican, and Jesuit missionaries undertook one of the most ambitious translation projects in human history.

They sat with indigenous elders. They documented grammars. They compiled vocabularies. They translated the Bible, the catechism, and the lives of the saints into Nahuatl, Quechua, Maya, Guaraní, and dozens of other languages — many of which had never been written down before.

Fray Bernardino de Sahagún produced the Florentine Codex — a twelve-volume encyclopedia of Aztec culture, history, and language compiled entirely through interviews with indigenous informants and written in both Nahuatl and Spanish. It remains one of the most important ethnographic documents ever created, and it was built entirely on the act of translation.

✦ ✦ ✦

What Was Lost — and What Survived

The conquest of the Americas was, among many other things, a linguistic catastrophe. Hundreds of languages disappeared entirely — spoken by peoples who were killed, enslaved, or absorbed into the colonial order.

And yet something extraordinary also happened. Spanish absorbed the Americas as much as the Americas were absorbed by Spanish. Thousands of indigenous words entered the language and traveled back across the ocean into everyday speech.

Chocolate  ·  Tomato  ·  Avocado  ·  Hurricane  ·  Canoe  ·  Barbecue

Every one of those words is indigenous American in origin. Every one of them traveled into Spanish — and then into English, and French, and dozens of other languages — through the work of interpreters and translators who found ways to carry meaning across the divide.

The conquest didn't just impose Spanish on the Americas. It also americanized Spanish. And the bridge between those two worlds — the fragile, imperfect, essential bridge — was always a translator.
