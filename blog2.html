When Arabic Shaped Spanish

The hidden language within a language — and what it means for translation.

When Arabic Shaped Spanish: The Hidden Language Within a Language

By Jimmy Cabrera  ·  Puente Translations
Bridging Languages. Connecting Lives.

If you've ever said the word ojalá — that beautiful Spanish expression of hope, somewhere between "I hope" and "God willing" — you've spoken Arabic.

You just didn't know it.

Ojalá comes from the Arabic inshallah — in sha' Allah — meaning "if God wills it." It crossed the Mediterranean over a thousand years ago, settled into the Spanish language, and never left. Today, millions of Spanish speakers use it every day without any idea that they're carrying a piece of Arabic with them.

This is the story of how that happened — and why it matters to anyone who works across languages.

✦ ✦ ✦

The Meeting of Two Worlds

In 711 AD, Arab and Berber forces crossed the Strait of Gibraltar and entered the Iberian Peninsula — the land that would become Spain and Portugal. They called it Al-Andalus. For nearly 800 years, Islamic civilization flourished there, producing some of the greatest achievements in mathematics, astronomy, medicine, philosophy, and literature the world had ever seen.

And for nearly 800 years, Arabic and the early forms of Spanish lived side by side.

They traded. They negotiated. They argued and debated. They built cities together — Toledo, Seville, Córdoba — that became the intellectual centers of the medieval world. Scholars from across Europe came to translate Arabic texts into Latin, unlocking centuries of knowledge that had been preserved and expanded by Islamic scholars while Europe struggled through its darker ages.

Language, as it always does when people live together, began to merge.

✦ ✦ ✦

The Words That Stayed

When the Reconquista — the Christian reconquest of Spain — was finally completed in 1492, the Arabic-speaking Muslims were expelled. But the language they left behind was not so easy to remove.

Linguists estimate that Spanish contains somewhere between 4,000 and 8,000 words of Arabic origin. Some of the most common words in Spanish come directly from Arabic:

Everyday Life

  • Almohada (pillow) — from Arabic al-mukhadda
  • Aceite (oil) — from Arabic az-zayt
  • Azúcar (sugar) — from Arabic as-sukkar
  • Alcalde (mayor) — from Arabic al-qadi (the judge)
  • Aldea (village) — from Arabic al-day'a

Agriculture & Nature

  • Naranja (orange) — from Arabic naranj
  • Algodón (cotton) — from Arabic al-qutn
  • Acequia (irrigation channel) — from Arabic as-saqiya

Science & Knowledge

  • Álgebra — directly from Arabic al-jabr
  • Algoritmo (algorithm) — from the Arab mathematician Al-Khwarizmi
  • Alquimia (alchemy) — from Arabic al-kimiya

Notice something about almost all of these words? They begin with al- or a-. That's the Arabic definite article — al means "the." When Arabic words entered Spanish, they often brought the article with them, fused permanently to the noun. The Spanish word for cotton isn't just qutn — it's al-qutn, with "the" baked in forever.

✦ ✦ ✦

The Language of Civilization

What made Arabic's influence on Spanish so deep wasn't just proximity — it was prestige.

During the height of Al-Andalus, Arabic was the language of science, philosophy, medicine, and poetry. It was the language of the most advanced civilization in the Western world. When Spanish speakers borrowed Arabic words, they weren't just filling vocabulary gaps — they were reaching for concepts and technologies that didn't exist in their own language yet.

Alcohol comes from Arabic al-kuhul. Cipher and zero both come from Arabic sifr. Admiral comes from Arabic amir al-bahr — "commander of the sea."

These aren't footnotes to history. They're the vocabulary of everyday modern life, carrying the memory of a civilization most people never think about.

✦ ✦ ✦

What This Means for Translation

Every language is a palimpsest — a manuscript that has been written over and over, with traces of older texts still visible beneath the surface.

When we translate Spanish into English, we're not just converting words from one column to another. We're moving meaning across layers of history, culture, and civilization. A Spanish document isn't just Spanish — it carries Visigothic grammar, Latin roots, Arabic vocabulary, and the poetry of the Golden Age, all compressed into the words a person chose to write on a page.

This is what makes translation genuinely difficult — and genuinely important.

When a Spanish-speaking family walks in with documents that need translating for USCIS, those papers aren't just bureaucratic paperwork. They're written in a language that carries 800 years of Arabic influence and the memory of one of history's greatest multicultural civilizations.

A translator's job is to carry all of that — carefully, accurately, and with respect for what language actually is.

✦ ✦ ✦

Ojalá

We'll end where we began.

Ojalá — borrowed from Arabic, shaped by centuries of contact between two great civilizations, now woven so deeply into Spanish that most speakers would be surprised to learn it isn't originally Spanish at all.

That's what language does. It absorbs. It transforms. It carries the past into the present without asking permission.

And somewhere in that process — in the movement of meaning from one world to another — translators have always been essential.

Ojalá that never changes.
